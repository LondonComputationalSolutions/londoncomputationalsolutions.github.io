Therapeutic Music

Over the course of the past two years, I have been working with pupils with additional needs, playing music with them, for them and to them. However, therapeutic music is used in various other contexts as well. Hospices, hospitals, elderly people's homes, care homes, mainstream schools and religious institutions have all reported about the benefits that therapeutic music and music interaction has on their charges. The effects of gently played music, in my case the harp, classical guitar, wooden flutes or the voice, can be described as a sound bath or gentle shower. Sound washes through and over the body, lowering blood pressure and heart beat and takes away muscular pains and tension. Song and rhythm can also increase oxygenation in the blood and stimulate the senses increasing attentiveness and communication which will be conducive in helping to orientate in the present. Generally, the recipient enters into a state of deep relaxation or flow, depending upon stimulation and atmosphere.

Apart from the more therapeutic approach to music, I also offer basic music lessons to people of all ages and abilities. I have experience teaching basic piano to medium to severe needs children and young adults. I have also taught the recorder and guitar and the Celtic harp. However with each individual it is always worth trying various instruments out first before settling on one in particular. In the case of special needs, if necessary, I try and make modifications to the various requirements that come with the individual instrument.

I have taught voice lessons to teenagers and adults, sometimes to people who feel the need to gain confidence with their voice; sometimes to aspiring pop or choir singers; and sometimes to people who simply enjoy learning and improving their own singing voice. I enjoy all of these teaching and singing contexts. It is always a very special situation to begin this journey of discovery of the voice with a person.
